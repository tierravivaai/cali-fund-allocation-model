# Near-term assessment of potential band movement

## Purpose

This note assesses whether a Party might move from its current IUSAF band in the near future. It combines two forms of evidence:

1. **Current threshold proximity**: how close a Party's current UN assessment rate is to the nearest band boundary.
2. **Historical band mobility**: whether the Party's approved UN assessment rate has actually crossed the current band thresholds over the last 20, 10 and 5 years.

The analysis does **not** predict future General Assembly decisions. It simply identifies where movement would be mechanically easier or harder if approved assessment rates were to change.

## Important framing

The IUSAF bands used in this model are **not United Nations bands**. They are analytical bands created for the model and applied to the UN scale of assessments.

The question is therefore not whether a Party is likely to move between official UN categories, because no such UN band categories exist. The question is whether the **model's own bands** appear to be:

1. **reasonable** in light of the distribution of UN-approved assessment rates; and
2. **stable** in the sense that Parties are unlikely to move between these analytical bands frequently over the near term.

This is why historical testing matters. If Parties rarely cross the current analytical thresholds when their approved UN assessment rates are tracked over time, that is evidence that the band structure is reasonably stable. If they cross often, that would suggest the thresholds may be too fragile.

This framing is especially important for **Band 6**. China was placed in Band 6 not because the United Nations created a separate category, but because its assessment rate is far above the Band 5 range and materially separated from Brazil, India and Mexico. The analytical issue is therefore whether that separate treatment remains justified and stable over time.

## General observation

The United Nations scale of assessments is a formal measure of contributions to the regular budget and is adopted by the General Assembly. That institutional process suggests that abrupt or frequent band movement should not be assumed.

The historical evidence is consistent with that view over a short horizon:

- over the last **20 years**, `61 of 139` currently eligible Parties crossed at least one of the current band thresholds;
- over the last **10 years**, `23 of 139` did so;
- over the last **5 years**, `10 of 139` did so.

This suggests that, for most Parties, **near-term band movement appears limited rather than routine**, while still remaining possible for a smaller set of Parties near thresholds or with a record of recent movement.

Taken together, these results support the view that the current analytical bands are broadly **stable enough to be workable**, while also showing that some thresholds are more sensitive than others.

## How to read the evidence

- A Party that is **close to a threshold today** but has **not moved historically** may be formally near a boundary without showing much recent empirical movement.
- A Party that is **close to a threshold today** and has **moved in the last 5 or 10 years** should be treated as a more plausible near-term crossover case.
- A Party that is **far from a threshold today** and has **not moved recently** can be treated as relatively stable for near-term purposes.

## Exact-threshold Parties

Many Parties sit exactly at the published thresholds of `0.001` or `0.010`. Under the implemented rule they remain in the lower band, but any movement in the upward direction would change their band assignment.

This should be interpreted carefully. Exact-threshold status means these Parties are **formally sensitive to any upward revision**, but it does **not** by itself establish that such a revision is likely in the near term. Historical stability remains important context.

In other words, exact-threshold cases are relevant to the question of **band design sensitivity**, but they do not automatically imply that the present band structure is unreasonable.

## Overall neutral conclusion

The available evidence supports the following neutral conclusion:

> The analytical bands used in the IUSAF model are not official UN bands; they are model-defined groupings applied to UN-approved assessment rates. On the available evidence, these analytical bands appear broadly reasonable and reasonably stable for near-term use: most Parties have not crossed them recently, and recent crossover is concentrated in a relatively small set of cases. A smaller group of Parties — particularly Guyana, Algeria and Cuba, and to a lesser extent India, Pakistan, Gabon, Brazil and China — may nevertheless merit closer monitoring because they are either close to a current threshold, have crossed thresholds historically, or both.

This formulation avoids assuming that any particular Party will move bands, while still identifying those for whom movement appears more plausible than for the broader set of Parties.
