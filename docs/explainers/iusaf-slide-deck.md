## The Inverted UN Scale of Assessments Formula

**Cali Fund explainer**

# The question

## How should the Cali Fund be shared fairly?

The formula needs to recognise:

- **economic capacity**
- **biodiversity stewardship**
- **Indigenous Peoples and local communities**

**Visual direction:** world map with forests, islands, oceans, and countries of different economic capacity.

<!--
Speaker notes:
The Cali Fund needs a transparent way to share money for biodiversity. Countries differ in their economic capacity and in the scale of the biodiversity stewardship responsibilities they carry.

# Start with a known scale

## The UN Scale of Assessments

The United Nations already uses a scale to help decide how much countries contribute to the UN budget.

```text
Higher economic capacity
        |
        v
Higher assessed contribution
```

**Visual direction:** a contribution ladder, with higher-capacity countries paying more.

<!--
Speaker notes:
The proposal starts from something familiar inside the UN system: the UN Scale of Assessments. The scale reflects economic capacity.
-->

---

# Turn the scale around

## From ability to pay to need for support

```text
UN budget use:
Economic capacity -> contribution

Cali Fund use:
Economic capacity -> support, inverted
```

Countries with lower assessed economic capacity receive larger shares.

**Visual direction:** contribution ladder flips into an allocation ladder.

<!--
Speaker notes:
The core move is simple. Instead of using the scale to ask how much a country should pay, the formula turns it around to ask how much support a country should receive.
-->

---

# The foundation

## IUSAF: the inverted UN Scale base

The main allocation comes from the inverted scale.

```text
Lower capacity countries  -> larger shares
Higher capacity countries -> smaller shares
```

The model uses **bands** to keep the result simple, transparent, and easier to explain.

**Visual direction:** six descending bands or steps.

<!--
Speaker notes:
The inverted UN Scale is the foundation. Countries are grouped into bands so that the formula is easier to communicate and avoids over-precision.
-->

---

# Add stewardship carefully

## Two small overlays

```text
Main base:  IUSAF  economic fairness

Small overlay: TSAC   land stewardship
Small overlay: SOSAC  ocean stewardship for SIDS
```

These overlays adjust the base. They do not replace it.

**Visual direction:** a large gold base bar, with small green and blue overlays.

<!--
Speaker notes:
The formula then adds two modest stewardship components. TSAC recognises terrestrial stewardship, using land area as a simple proxy. SOSAC recognises Small Island Developing States and their ocean stewardship responsibilities.
-->

---

# Keep the balance clear

## Most of the allocation remains in the fairness base

Under the balance points discussed in the paper:

- **about 94–95.5%** remains in the IUSAF base
- **about 4.5–6%** is the stewardship pool

**Visual direction:** mostly gold bar, with narrow green and blue sections.

```text
[ IUSAF base: 94–95.5% ][ stewardship: 4.5–6% ]
```

<!--
Speaker notes:
This is important. The stewardship components are policy choices, but they are small relative to the foundation. The model is mainly an economic fairness model with stewardship adjustments.
-->

---

# Build in communities

## At least half for Indigenous Peoples and local communities

Each geographic allocation is split into two channels:

```text
Country allocation
        |
        +-- State component
        |
        +-- Indigenous Peoples and local communities
```

**Visual direction:** each allocation divides into two equal baskets or channels.

<!--
Speaker notes:
The formula treats Indigenous Peoples and local communities as integral beneficiaries. At least half of each geographic allocation is directed to them, recognising their role in conservation and sustainable use.
-->

---

# The plain-language formula

## Fairness plus stewardship

```text
Country Share =
Inverted UN Scale
+ Terrestrial Stewardship
+ Ocean Stewardship
```

Or even more simply:

```text
Country Share = economic fairness + care for land + care for oceans
```

<!--
Speaker notes:
This is the simple version for public communication. The technical acronyms can be introduced later, but the main message is fairness plus stewardship.
-->

---

# The technical shorthand

```text
Country Share = IUSAF + TSAC + SOSAC
```

Where:

- **IUSAF** = inverted UN Scale base
- **TSAC** = terrestrial stewardship adjustment
- **SOSAC** = SIDS ocean stewardship adjustment

**Visual direction:** three labelled blocks, with IUSAF much larger than the others.

<!--
Speaker notes:
For technical audiences, the shorthand is IUSAF plus TSAC plus SOSAC. The key visual point is that IUSAF is the foundation, while TSAC and SOSAC are modest overlays.
-->

# One-minute narration

The Cali Fund needs a transparent way to share money for biodiversity.

This proposal starts with the UN Scale of Assessments, which reflects countries’ economic capacity.

It turns that scale around, so countries with lower assessed capacity receive larger shares.

Then it adds small adjustments for land and ocean stewardship.

At least half of each geographic allocation supports Indigenous Peoples and local communities.

**Fairness plus stewardship, with communities built in.**
