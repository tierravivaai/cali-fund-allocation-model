# Turning the Scale Upside Down

Audience: around 10 years old  
Format: 1–2 minute video, classroom slide, or narrated animation

## Core idea

The Cali Fund needs a fair way to share money for protecting nature. This proposal starts with a scale the United Nations already uses, turns it upside down, and then adds small extra amounts for countries that care for large land areas or big ocean spaces.

## Script

Imagine a classroom has a shared nature fund.

The question is: how should the money be shared fairly?

The United Nations already has a scale that helps decide how much countries pay into the UN budget. Countries with more money usually pay more.

The Cali Fund idea turns that scale upside down.

Instead of asking, “Who can pay more?”, it asks, “Who should receive more support?”

Countries with less economic capacity receive larger shares, because they may need more help to protect biodiversity.

That is the main part of the formula.

Then two small extras are added.

One extra recognises countries that look after very large land areas, such as forests, grasslands, and other habitats.

Another extra recognises Small Island Developing States, because many island countries look after large ocean spaces.

Finally, at least half of the money is for Indigenous Peoples and local communities. They are not added at the end. They are built into the model from the start.

So the simple version is:

Country share equals economic fairness, plus care for land, plus care for oceans.

## Simple formula

```text
Country Share = Economic Fairness + Land Stewardship + Ocean Stewardship
```

## Slide or scene plan

| Scene | Visual | Narration |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | A global piggy bank labelled “Cali Fund” | “The Cali Fund needs a fair way to share money for protecting nature.” |
| 2 | A UN contribution scale with richer countries placing more coins on it | “The UN already has a scale for deciding how much countries pay.” |
| 3 | The scale flips upside down and becomes a slide | “The Cali Fund idea turns that scale upside down.” |
| 4 | Six bands or steps, with lower-capacity countries receiving larger shares | “Countries with less economic capacity receive larger shares.” |
| 5 | A large base bar labelled “Main share” | “That is the main part of the formula.” |
| 6 | Small green overlay labelled “Land” | “One extra recognises countries that care for large land areas.” |
| 7 | Small blue overlay labelled “Ocean” | “Another extra recognises small island countries and ocean stewardship.” |
| 8 | A country share splits into two baskets: “State” and “IPLC” | “At least half is for Indigenous Peoples and local communities.” |
| 9 | Formula appears | “Country share equals economic fairness, plus care for land, plus care for oceans.” |

## Suggested visual style

- Use a flipped scale as the main image.
- Use green for land and blue for ocean.
- Use a clear 50/50 split graphic for Indigenous Peoples and local communities.
- Keep acronyms off the first version; introduce them only if needed.
